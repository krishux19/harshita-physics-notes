# BEGINNER EXPLANATION

Imagine you're holding an apple, and you let it go. What happens? It falls! Why? Because of something called **gravity**. You probably already know this, but have you ever wondered why the moon doesn't fall onto the Earth, or why the Earth doesn't fall into the Sun?

For thousands of years, people observed the sky. They saw stars that stayed in fixed positions, and then they saw "wanderers" – planets – that moved in peculiar paths.
*   **Ptolemy** (around 2000 years ago) thought everything revolved around the Earth (Geocentric model).
*   **Aryabhatta** (5th century AD) and later **Copernicus** (16th century) proposed a revolutionary idea: the Sun is the center, and planets revolve around it (Heliocentric model). This was a big deal!

Then came **Tycho Brahe**, who spent his life meticulously observing planets with just his eyes. His assistant, **Johannes Kepler**, analyzed all that data and discovered three amazing rules for how planets move:

1.  **Kepler's 1st Law (Law of Orbits):** Planets don't orbit in perfect circles, but in slightly stretched circles called **ellipses**. The Sun isn't exactly at the center, but at a special point called a "focus" of the ellipse. Imagine drawing an ellipse with two pins and a string – the pins are the foci.
2.  **Kepler's 2nd Law (Law of Areas):** A planet moves faster when it's closer to the Sun and slower when it's farther away. But here's the trick: the line connecting the planet to the Sun sweeps out the same amount of area in the same amount of time. Think of it like a clock hand – it sweeps equal areas, even if it moves at varying speeds.
3.  **Kepler's 3rd Law (Law of Periods):** There's a mathematical relationship between how long a planet takes to complete one orbit (its "period," T) and the size of its orbit (the "semi-major axis," 'a'). Specifically, T-squared is proportional to a-cubed (T² ∝ a³). This means bigger orbits take much longer!

Now, enter **Isaac Newton**. He saw an apple fall (the famous story!) and wondered: could the same force that pulls the apple down also keep the Moon in orbit around the Earth, and the Earth around the Sun? He realized it could! He connected the falling apple to the orbiting Moon, leading to his monumental discovery:

**Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:**
*   **Every object in the universe attracts every other object.** It's like a universal "pull."
*   This pull depends on **how massive** the objects are (bigger mass = stronger pull).
*   It also depends on **how far apart** they are (farther apart = weaker pull, specifically it decreases with the square of the distance).

So, the formula for this force is: `F = G * (m1 * m2) / r²`
*   `F` is the force.
*   `m1` and `m2` are the masses of the two objects.
*   `r` is the distance between their centers.
*   `G` is a very important constant, called the **Universal Gravitational Constant**. It's a tiny number (6.67 x 10⁻¹¹ Nm²/kg²), meaning gravity is usually a weak force unless masses are huge. **Henry Cavendish** was the first to accurately measure this tiny 'G' using a clever experiment with lead balls.

**Acceleration due to Gravity ('g'):**
When you drop an apple, it speeds up as it falls. This speeding up is called **acceleration**. On Earth's surface, this acceleration is approximately `9.8 m/s²`. We call this 'g'. It's caused by the Earth's gravity pulling on the apple.
*   Think of 'g' as the strength of Earth's gravitational field at a particular point.
*   'g' isn't constant everywhere!
    *   **Go higher (altitude):** 'g' decreases because you're farther from Earth's center.
    *   **Go deeper (depth):** 'g' also decreases because less of Earth's mass is "below" you pulling you down.
    *   **Earth's shape:** Earth isn't a perfect sphere; it bulges at the equator. So, 'g' is slightly less at the equator than at the poles.
    *   **Earth's rotation:** The spinning of the Earth also reduces 'g' slightly at the equator due to centrifugal effects.

**Gravitational Potential Energy:**
Imagine lifting the apple against gravity. You're doing work, and that work gets "stored" in the apple as **potential energy**. The higher you lift it, the more potential energy it gains.
*   For objects far from Earth, we define potential energy as `U = -GMm/r`. The negative sign might seem strange, but it just means gravity is an attractive force, and objects prefer to be closer. We say potential energy is zero when objects are infinitely far apart.
*   Close to Earth, this simplifies to the familiar `U = mgh`.

**Escape Speed:**
What if you throw the apple *really* hard? Could you throw it so hard that it never falls back down? Yes! The minimum speed required for an object to completely escape the gravitational pull of a celestial body and never return is called **escape speed**.
*   For Earth, this speed is about `11.2 km/s` (very fast!).
*   The Moon has a much lower escape speed (`2.3 km/s`), which is why it can't hold onto an atmosphere – gas molecules move fast enough to escape.

**Earth Satellites:**
These are objects (like the Moon or man-made satellites) that continuously fall around the Earth without hitting it. They're basically in a constant state of "free fall" around the Earth.
*   They need a specific **orbital velocity** to stay in orbit. If they go too slow, they fall; too fast, they escape.
*   The **total energy** of an orbiting satellite is always negative. This means it's "bound" to the Earth. If its energy were zero or positive, it would escape.

This chapter is about understanding these fundamental rules that govern everything from a falling apple to the dance of galaxies!

# COMPLETE CONCEPT NOTES

## 7.1 Introduction

*   **Definition:** Gravitation is the fundamental force of attraction between any two objects possessing mass. It explains why objects fall to the Earth, the motion of planets around the Sun, and the behavior of celestial bodies.
*   **Intuition:** The invisible "pull" that brings things down to Earth. It's the reason you feel weight, and why planets stay in their paths.
*   **Why it matters:** It's one of the four fundamental forces of nature, governing the structure of the universe from atoms to galaxies. For NEET, it underpins concepts like potential energy, orbital mechanics, and fields.
*   **Real world examples:**
    *   An apple falling from a tree.
    *   Raindrops falling to the Earth.
    *   The Moon orbiting the Earth.
    *   The Earth orbiting the Sun.
    *   Tides in oceans (due to Moon's and Sun's gravity).
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Galileo Galilei (1564-1642):** Recognized that all bodies, irrespective of their masses, accelerate towards the Earth with a constant acceleration (in the absence of air resistance). Public demonstrations and inclined plane experiments validated this.
    *   **Early Astronomical Models:**
        *   **Geocentric Model (Ptolemy, ~2000 years ago):** Earth at the center, all celestial bodies (Sun, planets, stars) revolve around it in circles. Complex schemes (epicycles) were proposed to explain observed planetary motions.
        *   **Heliocentric Model (Aryabhatta, 5th century A.D.; Nicolas Copernicus, 1473-1543):** Sun at the center, planets revolve around it. Copernicus initially proposed circular orbits.
    *   **Tycho Brahe (1546-1601):** Collected extensive, accurate naked-eye observations of planetary motions. His data became the foundation for Kepler's laws.

## 7.2 Kepler's Laws

*   **Definition:** Three empirical laws describing the motion of planets around the Sun, derived by Johannes Kepler from Tycho Brahe's observational data.
*   **Intuition:** How planets actually move, not just "in circles." They move faster when closer to the sun and sweep out "fair" areas over time.
*   **Why it matters:** These laws accurately describe planetary motion and were crucial for Newton's formulation of the universal law of gravitation. They are directly tested in NEET.
*   **Real world examples:** Motion of all planets, comets, and even artificial satellites around their central bodies.
*   **Important observations:**

### 1. Law of Orbits (Kepler's First Law)
    *   **Definition:** All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun situated at one of the foci of the ellipse.
    *   **Intuition:** Planets follow slightly "stretched" circular paths. The Sun isn't exactly in the middle but a bit off-center.
    *   **Important terms:**
        *   **Ellipse:** A closed curve where the sum of the distances from any point on the curve to two fixed points (foci) is constant. A circle is a special case of an ellipse where the two foci coincide.
        *   **Foci (F1, F2):** The two fixed points within an ellipse. The Sun is at one focus.
        *   **Perihelion (P):** The point in the orbit where the planet is closest to the Sun.
        *   **Aphelion (A):** The point in the orbit where the planet is farthest from the Sun.
        *   **Semi-major axis (a):** Half the length of the longest diameter of the ellipse. For a circular orbit, 'a' is the radius.
    *   **Why it matters:** Corrected Copernicus's idea of circular orbits. It sets the stage for understanding the varying speeds of planets.

### 2. Law of Areas (Kepler's Second Law)
    *   **Definition:** The line joining any planet to the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time.
    *   **Intuition:** Planets "cover ground" faster when they are nearer the Sun and slower when they are farther. The "area swept" by the planet-Sun line is constant over time.
    *   **Important observations:**
        *   **Consequence of Conservation of Angular Momentum:** This law arises directly from the fact that the gravitational force is a central force. For any central force, the torque acting on the planet about the Sun is zero, thus angular momentum (L = r x p = r x mv) is conserved.
        *   **Mathematical Representation:** dA/dt = L / (2m) = constant.
        *   **Speed Variation:** Since the area swept is proportional to (r x v), when r is small (near perihelion), v must be large to keep dA/dt constant. Conversely, when r is large (near aphelion), v is small.
    *   **Why it matters:** Explains why planets speed up and slow down in their orbits. Crucial link to angular momentum conservation.

### 3. Law of Periods (Kepler's Third Law)
    *   **Definition:** The square of the time period of revolution (T) of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (a) of its elliptical orbit.
    *   **Intuition:** Bigger orbits take disproportionately longer. If an orbit is twice as big, it takes more than twice as long to complete.
    *   **Mathematical Representation:** T² ∝ a³. For circular orbits, 'a' is simply the radius 'R'. So, T² ∝ R³.
    *   **Important observations:**
        *   The constant of proportionality (T²/a³) is the same for all planets orbiting the same central body (e.g., all planets around the Sun).
        *   This law was later derived by Newton from his Law of Universal Gravitation, providing strong evidence for his theory.
    *   **Why it matters:** Allows prediction of orbital periods or distances. Heavily tested in NEET, often for comparing different planets or satellites.

## 7.3 Universal Law of Gravitation

*   **Definition:** Every body in the universe attracts every other body with a force which is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
*   **Intuition:** Everything pulls on everything else, and this pull depends on how much "stuff" they have and how far apart they are.
*   **Why it matters:** The cornerstone of classical mechanics, explaining both terrestrial gravity and celestial motion. Essential for almost all calculations in this chapter.
*   **Real world examples:** The force between Earth and a satellite, the force between two human beings (extremely small), the force between galaxies.

### Scalar Form:
    *   `F = G * (m1 * m2) / r²`
    *   `F`: Magnitude of the gravitational force.
    *   `G`: Universal Gravitational Constant.
    *   `m1`, `m2`: Masses of the two interacting bodies.
    *   `r`: Distance between the centers of the two bodies.

### Vector Form:
    *   `**F**_12 = - G * (m1 * m2) / r² * **r̂**_12`
        *   `**F**_12`: Force on mass `m1` due to `m2`.
        *   `**r̂**_12`: Unit vector pointing from `m1` to `m2`.
        *   The negative sign indicates that the force is attractive, i.e., `**F**_12` is directed opposite to `**r̂**_12`, pulling `m1` towards `m2`.
    *   By Newton's Third Law, `**F**_12 = - **F**_21`. Gravitational forces form action-reaction pairs.

### Important observations:
*   **Universal Nature:** Applicable to all bodies, regardless of their size, composition, or state of motion.
*   **Always Attractive:** Gravitational force is always a pulling force, never a pushing force.
*   **Central Force:** The force acts along the line joining the centers of the two bodies.
*   **Inverse Square Law:** The force decreases rapidly with distance. Doubling the distance reduces the force to one-fourth.
*   **Weakest Force:** Gravitation is the weakest of the four fundamental forces.
*   **Principle of Superposition:** For a system of multiple particles, the net gravitational force on any one particle is the vector sum of the gravitational forces exerted by all other individual particles.
    *   `**F**_net = **F**_1 + **F**_2 + ... + **F**_n`
    *   Each force is calculated independently.

### Gravitational Force by Spherical Bodies:
*   **Uniform Spherical Shell (Hollow Sphere):**
    1.  **Point mass outside:** The shell attracts the point mass as if its entire mass were concentrated at its center. `F = G * (M * m) / r²` (where `r` is distance from center of shell).
    2.  **Point mass inside:** The gravitational force on a point mass situated anywhere inside a uniform spherical shell is **zero**. This is a crucial result derived from symmetry and calculus.
*   **Uniform Solid Sphere:**
    1.  **Point mass outside:** The solid sphere attracts the point mass as if its entire mass were concentrated at its center. `F = G * (M * m) / r²` (where `r` is distance from center of sphere).
    2.  **Point mass inside:** The force on a point mass inside a uniform solid sphere is exerted *only* by the mass of the sphere that is *closer to the center than the point mass*. The outer shell of mass does not contribute to the force. `F = G * (M_r * m) / r²`, where `M_r` is the mass of the sphere of radius `r`. If density `ρ` is uniform, `M_r = M_total * (r³/R_total³)`. This leads to `F ∝ r` (force is directly proportional to distance from center).

## 7.4 The Gravitational Constant (G)

*   **Definition:** The constant of proportionality in Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation, representing the strength of the gravitational interaction.
*   **Intuition:** It's a tiny number that tells us how weak gravity fundamentally is, meaning you need huge masses to feel a significant gravitational pull.
*   **Why it matters:** Allows quantitative calculation of gravitational force. Its measurement was a landmark achievement.
*   **Value and Units:** `G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m²/kg²` (SI units).
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Universal Constant:** Its value is believed to be the same throughout the universe.
    *   **Dimensional Formula:** `[M⁻¹ L³ T⁻²]`.
    *   **Cavendish Experiment (1798):**
        *   **Method:** Used a torsion balance (a delicate apparatus with small lead spheres attached to a suspended bar, attracted by larger lead spheres).
        *   **Principle:** The gravitational torque between the spheres caused the wire to twist. By measuring the angle of twist and knowing the torsion constant of the wire, Cavendish could determine the gravitational force. Since masses and distances were known, G could be calculated.
        *   **Significance:** First accurate measurement of G. Enabled calculation of Earth's mass, hence "Cavendish weighed the Earth."

## 7.5 Acceleration due to Gravity of the Earth ('g')

*   **Definition:** The acceleration experienced by an object due to the gravitational force of the Earth. It's also known as gravitational field intensity.
*   **Intuition:** How fast things speed up when they fall freely near Earth's surface.
*   **Why it matters:** Crucial for understanding weight, projectile motion, and orbital mechanics near Earth. Often directly asked in NEET.
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Relation to Force:** From Newton's second law, `F = mg`. From universal gravitation, `F = G * (M_E * m) / R_E²`.
    *   Therefore, `mg = G * (M_E * m) / R_E²`, which implies `g = G * M_E / R_E²`.
    *   `M_E`: Mass of the Earth (`~6 × 10²⁴ kg`).
    *   `R_E`: Radius of the Earth (`~6.4 × 10⁶ m`).
    *   **Value on Earth's Surface:** `g ≈ 9.8 m/s²`.
    *   **Independence:** 'g' is independent of the mass of the falling object (Galileo's observation).

## 7.6 Acceleration due to Gravity Below and Above the Surface of Earth

*   **Intuition:** 'g' isn't constant; it changes depending on where you are relative to Earth's center.
*   **Why it matters:** Explains why things weigh slightly less on mountaintops or in deep mines. Frequently tested in NEET, often requiring comparisons.

### 1. Variation with Height (h) above the Surface:
    *   **Concept:** As you go higher, you are farther from the Earth's center, so the gravitational pull weakens.
    *   **Formula:** At height `h`, distance from center is `(R_E + h)`.
        *   `g(h) = G * M_E / (R_E + h)²`
        *   `g(h) = g * [R_E² / (R_E + h)²] = g * [R_E / (R_E + h)]²`
        *   `g(h) = g * [1 / (1 + h/R_E)²]`
    *   **Approximation for small h (h << R_E):** Using binomial expansion `(1+x)^-2 ≈ 1-2x` for small x.
        *   `g(h) ≈ g * (1 - 2h/R_E)`
    *   **Observation:** 'g' decreases with increasing height. The decrease is linear for small heights.

### 2. Variation with Depth (d) below the Surface:
    *   **Concept:** When you go below the surface, only the mass of the Earth closer to the center than your position contributes to the gravitational force. The outer spherical shell of mass exerts zero net force.
    *   **Formula:** At depth `d`, distance from center is `(R_E - d)`. Assume uniform density `ρ`.
        *   `M_effective = (4/3) * π * (R_E - d)³ * ρ`
        *   `M_E = (4/3) * π * R_E³ * ρ`
        *   `g(d) = G * M_effective / (R_E - d)² = G * [(4/3) * π * (R_E - d)³ * ρ] / (R_E - d)²`
        *   `g(d) = G * (4/3) * π * (R_E - d) * ρ`
        *   Since `g = G * (4/3) * π * R_E * ρ` (from `g = G * M_E / R_E²`), we can write:
        *   `g(d) = g * (R_E - d) / R_E = g * (1 - d/R_E)`
    *   **Observation:** 'g' decreases linearly with increasing depth.
    *   **At the center of Earth (d = R_E):** `g(0) = g * (1 - R_E/R_E) = 0`. So, `g` is zero at the Earth's center.

### 3. Variation with Shape of Earth (Oblate Spheroid): (NEET-specific extension)
    *   **Concept:** Earth is not a perfect sphere; it's an oblate spheroid (flattened at the poles, bulges at the equator) due to its rotation.
    *   **Observation:**
        *   Radius at poles (`R_P`) is less than radius at equator (`R_E`).
        *   Since `g = GM/R²`, and `R_P < R_E`, `g_P > g_E` (gravitational acceleration is higher at poles).

### 4. Variation with Rotation of Earth: (NEET-specific extension)
    *   **Concept:** Due to Earth's rotation, a body at the equator experiences a centrifugal force outwards, effectively reducing its apparent weight and thus the effective 'g'.
    *   **Formula:** At latitude `λ`, the effective acceleration due to gravity `g'` is given by:
        *   `g' = g - ω²R_E * cos²λ`
        *   `g`: Actual acceleration due to gravity if Earth were not rotating.
        *   `ω`: Angular velocity of Earth's rotation.
        *   `λ`: Latitude of the location.
    *   **Observation:**
        *   **At Poles (λ = 90°):** `cos 90° = 0`, so `g' = g`. No effect of rotation.
        *   **At Equator (λ = 0°):** `cos 0° = 1`, so `g' = g - ω²R_E`. This is the maximum reduction in 'g'.
        *   'g' is minimum at the equator and maximum at the poles due to both rotation and shape.

## 7.7 Gravitational Potential Energy (U)

*   **Definition:** The energy stored in a body by virtue of its position in a gravitational field. It is the work done by an external agent to bring the body from infinity (where U=0) to its current position without acceleration.
*   **Intuition:** Stored energy due to height or position. Objects naturally "want" to move to lower potential energy states (e.g., fall down).
*   **Why it matters:** Critical for energy conservation problems, escape velocity, and satellite motion. NEET loves energy conservation.
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Conservative Force:** Gravitational force is a conservative force, meaning the work done by it (or against it) depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken. This allows us to define potential energy.
    *   **Reference Point:** We conventionally choose the gravitational potential energy `U = 0` at infinity (`r = ∞`).
    *   **Formula:** `U = - G * M * m / r`
        *   `M`: Mass of the central body creating the field.
        *   `m`: Mass of the object in the field.
        *   `r`: Distance between the centers of `M` and `m`.
    *   **Always Negative:** Since gravity is attractive, work is done *by* the field as `m` approaches `M`. Starting from `U=0` at infinity, the potential energy becomes negative as `r` decreases. A negative potential energy indicates a bound system.
    *   **Potential Energy Difference:** The work done in moving a particle from `r1` to `r2` is `W_12 = U(r2) - U(r1) = GMm (1/r1 - 1/r2)`.
    *   **Relation to mgh (approximation):** Near Earth's surface, for small height differences `h`, the general formula `U = -GMm/r` approximates to `U = mgh` (relative to a local reference point).
        *   `U_h - U_0 = [-GMm/(R+h)] - [-GMm/R] = GMm [(1/R) - 1/(R+h)] = GMm * [h / (R(R+h))]`
        *   For `h << R`, `R(R+h) ≈ R²`. So, `GMm * h/R² = (GM/R²) * mh = g * mh = mgh`.
    *   **Potential Energy of a System of Particles:** For a system of `n` particles, the total potential energy is the sum of the potential energies of all possible distinct pairs of particles.
        *   `U_total = Σ_pairs (-G * m_i * m_j / r_ij)`

### Gravitational Potential (V)
    *   **Definition:** The gravitational potential at a point in a gravitational field is the gravitational potential energy per unit mass at that point. It is the work done by an external agent in bringing a unit mass from infinity to that point without acceleration.
    *   **Intuition:** A map of "gravity's strength" at different locations, independent of the mass you're placing there.
    *   **Formula:** `V = U / m = - G * M / r`
    *   **Units:** Joules per kilogram (J/kg).
    *   **Scalar Quantity:** Potential is a scalar quantity.
    *   **Potential Difference:** `ΔV = V_final - V_initial`. The work done in moving mass `m` from `A` to `B` is `W_AB = m * (V_B - V_A)`.
    *   **Why it matters:** Simplifies calculations for multiple masses and work done in a field.

## 7.8 Escape Speed (Escape Velocity)

*   **Definition:** The minimum speed with which a body must be projected from the surface of a planet (or any gravitational field) so that it just escapes the gravitational field and never returns. At infinity, its kinetic energy becomes zero.
*   **Intuition:** How fast you need to throw something to make it fly away forever, never to come back.
*   **Why it matters:** Explains why planets have atmospheres (or don't), crucial for space missions, and a common NEET problem type.
*   **Derivation (using Conservation of Mechanical Energy):**
    *   Initial state (on surface): `KE_i = (1/2)mv_e²`, `PE_i = -GMm/R_E`
    *   Final state (at infinity, just escaped): `KE_f = 0`, `PE_f = 0`
    *   `KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f`
    *   `(1/2)mv_e² - GMm/R_E = 0 + 0`
    *   `(1/2)mv_e² = GMm/R_E`
    *   `v_e² = 2GM/R_E`
    *   `v_e = √(2GM/R_E)`
*   **Alternative Form:** Since `g = GM/R_E²`, we have `GM = gR_E²`.
    *   `v_e = √(2gR_E)`
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Independence from Mass of Projectile:** Escape speed depends only on the mass and radius of the planet from which the body is escaping, not on the mass of the projectile itself.
    *   **Independence from Direction:** It's a scalar quantity. The direction of projection does not affect the escape speed, only whether the object *actually* escapes. However, practically, for escape, the initial velocity vector should not point directly into the planet.
    *   **Independence from Angle of Projection:** Once the minimum speed is achieved, the angle doesn't matter for *escaping*.
    *   **Dependence on Location Height:** If projected from a height `h` above the surface, `R_E` in the formula is replaced by `(R_E + h)`.
    *   **Earth's Escape Speed:** `v_e ≈ 11.2 km/s`.
    *   **Moon's Escape Speed:** `v_e ≈ 2.3 km/s`. This lower value explains why the Moon has no atmosphere; gas molecules can easily attain speeds greater than `v_e`.
    *   **Beyond Escape Speed:** If a body is projected with speed `v > v_e`, it will escape to infinity with some residual kinetic energy. Its speed at infinity `v_∞` can be found from `(1/2)mv_∞² = (1/2)mv² - GMm/R`.

## 7.9 Earth Satellites

*   **Definition:** Objects (natural or artificial) that revolve around the Earth due to its gravitational pull. Their motion follows Kepler's laws.
*   **Intuition:** Artificial moons that orbit Earth, like the real moon. They're constantly falling towards Earth but moving sideways fast enough to keep missing it.
*   **Why it matters:** Crucial for telecommunication, weather forecasting, GPS, space exploration. Highly relevant for NEET orbital mechanics problems.
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **Natural Satellite:** Moon.
    *   **Artificial Satellites:** Launched by humans (e.g., Sputnik, INSAT, GSAT series).
    *   **Circular Orbits (Simplification):** Often analyzed as circular for easier calculations.

### Orbital Velocity (v_o)
    *   **Definition:** The horizontal speed required for a satellite to maintain a stable circular orbit at a specific height `h` above the Earth's surface.
    *   **Derivation:** Gravitational force provides the necessary centripetal force.
        *   `F_gravitational = G * M_E * m / (R_E + h)²`
        *   `F_centripetal = m * v_o² / (R_E + h)`
        *   Equating them: `G * M_E * m / (R_E + h)² = m * v_o² / (R_E + h)`
        *   `v_o² = G * M_E / (R_E + h)`
        *   `v_o = √(G * M_E / (R_E + h))`
    *   **For a satellite very close to Earth's surface (h ≈ 0):**
        *   `v_o = √(G * M_E / R_E) = √(g * R_E)` (since `GM_E = gR_E²`)
        *   `v_o ≈ 7.92 km/s`. This is approximately `v_e / √2`.
    *   **Observation:** Orbital velocity decreases as height `h` increases.

### Time Period of Satellite (T)
    *   **Definition:** The time taken for a satellite to complete one full revolution around the Earth.
    *   **Derivation:** `T = Circumference / v_o = 2π(R_E + h) / v_o`
        *   Substitute `v_o = √(G * M_E / (R_E + h))`:
        *   `T = 2π(R_E + h) / √(G * M_E / (R_E + h))`
        *   `T = 2π * √[(R_E + h)³ / (G * M_E)]`
    *   **Squaring both sides (Kepler's Third Law for satellites):**
        *   `T² = (4π² / G * M_E) * (R_E + h)³`
        *   Here, `k = 4π² / G * M_E` is the constant of proportionality, which is the same for all satellites orbiting Earth.
    *   **Observation:** Time period increases as height `h` increases.

### Geostationary Satellites (Special Case of Satellites) (NEET-specific extension)
    *   **Definition:** A satellite that appears stationary relative to an observer on the Earth's surface.
    *   **Conditions:**
        1.  **Orbital Period:** `T = 24 hours` (same as Earth's rotation period).
        2.  **Orbital Direction:** Orbits in the same direction as Earth's rotation (west to east).
        3.  **Orbital Plane:** Orbits in the equatorial plane.
        4.  **Height:** Unique fixed height (`h ≈ 36,000 km` or `r ≈ 42,400 km` from Earth's center).
    *   **Applications:** Telecommunications, TV broadcasting, weather monitoring.

## 7.10 Energy of an Orbiting Satellite

*   **Intuition:** A satellite has both movement energy (kinetic) and position energy (potential) which combine to give its total energy.
*   **Why it matters:** Determines if a satellite is bound or will escape. Energy changes are important for orbit changes. Highly tested in NEET.

### For a circular orbit at radius `r = (R_E + h)`:
1.  **Kinetic Energy (KE):** `KE = (1/2)mv_o²`
    *   Substitute `v_o² = G * M_E / r`:
    *   `KE = G * M_E * m / (2r)`
    *   **Always positive.**

2.  **Potential Energy (PE):** `PE = - G * M_E * m / r`
    *   **Always negative** (assuming `PE=0` at infinity).

3.  **Total Mechanical Energy (E):** `E = KE + PE`
    *   `E = (G * M_E * m / (2r)) + (- G * M_E * m / r)`
    *   `E = - G * M_E * m / (2r)`
    *   **Always negative** for a bound orbit. This means the satellite is "bound" to the Earth.
    *   **Relation:** `E = -KE = PE/2`. (This is a very important relation for circular orbits).

### Binding Energy
    *   **Definition:** The energy required to remove a satellite from its orbit to infinity (i.e., to make it escape the Earth's gravitational influence).
    *   **Formula:** `Binding Energy = - (Total Mechanical Energy) = G * M_E * m / (2r)`
    *   **Intuition:** It's the "energy deficit" that keeps the satellite in orbit. To free it, you need to supply this much positive energy.

### Important observations:
*   **Negative Total Energy:** For any bound system (like an orbiting satellite or a planet around the Sun), the total mechanical energy is negative.
*   **Positive Total Energy:** If the total energy is positive or zero, the object will escape to infinity.
*   **Elliptical Orbits:** For elliptical orbits, KE and PE vary, but the total energy remains constant and negative. The relation `E = -KE = PE/2` only holds for circular orbits.
*   **Changing Orbit:** To change a satellite's orbit (e.g., to a higher orbit), its total energy must be increased (made less negative). This requires providing additional energy, usually by firing rockets.

## Weightlessness

*   **Definition:** The apparent absence of weight, experienced by an object when the net contact force (or normal force) supporting it is zero.
*   **Intuition:** The feeling of floating, like astronauts in space.
*   **Why it matters:** A common misconception is that weightlessness means zero gravity. NEET often tests this.
*   **Important observations:**
    *   **NOT Zero Gravity:** In an orbiting space station, there is still significant gravitational pull from Earth. If not, the station wouldn't orbit!
    *   **Free Fall:** Weightlessness occurs because both the astronaut and the space station are constantly falling around the Earth together. There's no relative motion, and thus no normal force or contact force to "feel" weight.
    *   **Example:** An elevator cable breaks – for a moment, you'd feel weightless inside because both you and the elevator are accelerating downwards at 'g'.
    *   **Large Space Station:** Even in a large space station, a very sensitive instrument might detect a slight gravitational gradient (tidal effect) because different parts of the station are at slightly different distances from Earth and thus experience slightly different gravitational forces. However, for a human, the sensation of weightlessness persists.

# DERIVATIONS AND LOGIC

### 1. Kepler's Second Law (Law of Areas) from Conservation of Angular Momentum

**Intuition:** Imagine a planet orbiting the Sun. If the force pulling it (gravity) always points directly towards the Sun, then that force can't make the planet spin faster or slower around the Sun. This "spin" is angular momentum, and if there's no force causing it to change, it stays constant. The law of areas is just a mathematical way of saying this "spin" (angular momentum) is conserved.

**Derivation:**
1.  **Define Angular Momentum:** For a particle of mass `m` at position vector `**r**` with momentum `**p** = m**v**`, its angular momentum `**L**` about the origin (where the Sun is located) is given by `**L** = **r** × **p** = **r** × (m**v**)` (Equation 7.2).
2.  **Torque and Angular Momentum:** The rate of change of angular momentum is equal to the net external torque `**τ**` acting on the particle: `d**L**/dt = **τ**`.
3.  **Central Force:** Gravitational force is a central force, meaning it always acts along the line joining the planet and the Sun. If the Sun is at the origin, the force `**F**` is always parallel (or anti-parallel, being attractive) to the position vector `**r**`.
4.  **Zero Torque:** The torque `**τ**` due to a central force is `**τ** = **r** × **F**`. Since `**r**` and `**F**` are parallel (or anti-parallel), their cross product is zero (`**r** × **F** = 0`).
5.  **Conservation:** Since `d**L**/dt = 0`, the angular momentum `**L**` is constant.
6.  **Relating Angular Momentum to Area:**
    *   Consider a small time interval `Δt`. The planet moves from `**r**` to `**r'** = **r** + `**v**Δt`.
    *   The area `ΔA` swept out by the radius vector `**r**` is approximately the area of the triangle formed by `**r**`, `**r'**`, and the origin.
    *   `ΔA = (1/2) |**r** × **r'**| = (1/2) |**r** × (**r** + **v**Δt)|`
    *   `ΔA = (1/2) |**r** × **r** + **r** × **v**Δt| = (1/2) |0 + (**r** × **v**)Δt|`
    *   `ΔA = (1/2) |**r** × **v**| Δt`
    *   Divide by `Δt`: `dA/dt = (1/2) |**r** × **v**|`
7.  **Final Connection:** We know `**L** = m (**r** × **v**)`, so `|**r** × **v**| = |**L**| / m`.
    *   Therefore, `dA/dt = (1/2) * (|**L**| / m)`.
    *   Since `L` and `m` are constant, `dA/dt` is constant. This is Kepler's Second Law.

### 2. Universal Law of Gravitation from Kepler's Third Law (Intuitive Connection, as Newton did)

**Intuition:** Newton didn't derive Kepler's laws first. Instead, he *used* Kepler's Third Law (and his own laws of motion) to *infer* the inverse square nature of gravity. He saw that if gravity followed an inverse square law, Kepler's Third Law would naturally emerge.

**Logic (Newton's Reasoning):**
1.  **Assume Circular Orbits:** For simplicity, Newton considered circular orbits (which are a good approximation for most planets).
2.  **Centripetal Force:** For a planet of mass `m` orbiting the Sun of mass `M_S` in a circle of radius `R` with speed `V`, the centripetal force required is `F_c = mV²/R`.
3.  **Orbital Velocity and Period:** The orbital speed `V = 2πR/T`. Substituting this into `F_c`:
    *   `F_c = m(2πR/T)² / R = m(4π²R²/T²) / R = 4π²mR / T²`.
4.  **Kepler's Third Law:** From observations, `T² = kR³` (where `k` is a constant for all planets orbiting the Sun).
5.  **Substitute Kepler's Law:** Replace `T²` in the `F_c` equation:
    *   `F_c = 4π²mR / (kR³) = (4π²m / k) * (1/R²)`.
6.  **Infer Inverse Square Law:** This shows that the force required to keep a planet in orbit varies as `1/R²`. Newton hypothesized that this centripetal force was the gravitational force, and that it must also be proportional to the masses involved. Thus, he proposed `F = G * (m_1 * m_2) / R²`.

### 3. Escape Speed Derivation

**Intuition:** To escape Earth's gravity, an object needs enough initial kinetic energy to overcome all the negative gravitational potential energy it has when on Earth's surface. At infinity, its speed (and thus KE) will be zero, and its PE will also be zero (by convention).

**Derivation (using Conservation of Mechanical Energy):**
1.  **Initial State (on Earth's surface):**
    *   Mass of projectile = `m`
    *   Mass of Earth = `M_E`
    *   Radius of Earth = `R_E`
    *   Initial speed = `v_e` (escape speed)
    *   Initial Kinetic Energy (`KE_i`) = `(1/2)mv_e²`
    *   Initial Potential Energy (`PE_i`) = `-GM_E m / R_E` (taking `PE=0` at infinity)
    *   Total Initial Energy (`E_i`) = `(1/2)mv_e² - GM_E m / R_E`
2.  **Final State (just escaped, at infinity):**
    *   Final speed = `0` (minimum speed for escape means it just stops at infinity)
    *   Final Kinetic Energy (`KE_f`) = `0`
    *   Final Potential Energy (`PE_f`) = `0` (by definition of reference point)
    *   Total Final Energy (`E_f`) = `0`
3.  **Conservation of Mechanical Energy:** `E_i = E_f`
    *   `(1/2)mv_e² - GM_E m / R_E = 0`
    *   `(1/2)mv_e² = GM_E m / R_E`
    *   `v_e² = 2GM_E / R_E`
    *   `v_e = √(2GM_E / R_E)`
4.  **Alternative form using 'g':**
    *   We know `g = GM_E / R_E²`, so `GM_E = gR_E²`.
    *   Substituting this into the `v_e` formula:
    *   `v_e = √(2 * gR_E² / R_E) = √(2gR_E)`

### 4. Orbital Velocity and Energy of a Circularly Orbiting Satellite

**Intuition:** A satellite stays in orbit because the Earth's gravity constantly pulls it towards the center, providing exactly the right force needed to keep it moving in a circle. This inward pull is the centripetal force. Its energy is a balance of its motion (KE) and its position in the gravitational field (PE).

**Derivation (Orbital Velocity `v_o`):**
1.  **Balance of Forces:** For a satellite of mass `m` orbiting Earth (mass `M_E`) in a circular orbit of radius `r` (where `r = R_E + h`, `h` is height), the gravitational force provides the centripetal force.
    *   Gravitational Force: `F_G = G M_E m / r²`
    *   Centripetal Force: `F_c = m v_o² / r`
2.  **Equating Forces:** `G M_E m / r² = m v_o² / r`
3.  **Solving for `v_o`:**
    *   `v_o² = G M_E / r`
    *   `v_o = √(G M_E / r)`

**Derivation (Energy Components):**
1.  **Kinetic Energy (KE):**
    *   `KE = (1/2)mv_o²`
    *   Substitute `v_o² = G M_E / r`:
    *   `KE = (1/2)m (G M_E / r) = G M_E m / (2r)`
2.  **Potential Energy (PE):**
    *   Using the convention `PE = 0` at infinity:
    *   `PE = - G M_E m / r`
3.  **Total Mechanical Energy (E):**
    *   `E = KE + PE`
    *   `E = (G M_E m / (2r)) + (- G M_E m / r)`
    *   `E = (G M_E m / r) * (1/2 - 1)`
    *   `E = - G M_E m / (2r)`

**Important Relationship:** From the energy derivations, observe:
*   `E = -KE`
*   `PE = 2E`
*   `PE = -2KE`

These relations are extremely useful for quick problem-solving in NEET for circular orbits.

### 5. Spherical Shell Theorems (Qualitative Logic)

**Intuition:** Imagine being inside a hollow sphere. The material above you pulls you up, the material below you pulls you down, and the material to your sides pulls you sideways. But because it's perfectly symmetrical, all these pulls cancel out perfectly. If you're outside, it's like all the mass is squished into a tiny point at the center.

**Logic for Outside Point:**
1.  Consider a point mass `m` outside a spherical shell of mass `M` and radius `R`.
2.  Divide the shell into many small rings. The force from each ring has components along the line joining the point mass to the shell's center and perpendicular to it.
3.  Due to symmetry, all perpendicular components cancel out when summed over the entire shell.
4.  Only the components along the line to the center remain.
5.  Calculus shows that the sum of these remaining components is exactly the same as if the entire mass `M` were concentrated at the center of the shell.

**Logic for Inside Point:**
1.  Consider a point mass `m` inside a spherical shell of mass `M` and radius `R`.
2.  Draw two infinitesimal cones with the point mass `m` at their apexes, extending in opposite directions to intersect the shell.
3.  These cones cut out two small areas of the shell. The gravitational force due to these areas will be inversely proportional to the square of the distance from `m` to the area, and directly proportional to the mass of the area.
4.  Because of the inverse square law, if the cone is wider (further away) it encompasses more mass, exactly balancing the weaker force due to distance.
5.  Through integration over the entire shell, it is found that the forces from opposite sides cancel each other out precisely, resulting in zero net force inside the shell.

# NEET HIGH-YIELD CONCEPTS

These are the concepts that are most frequently tested or are very likely to appear in NEET. Focus your study and practice heavily on these.

1.  **Kepler's Third Law (T² ∝ a³):**
    *   **Applications:** Comparing periods/radii for planets around the Sun, or satellites around Earth.
    *   **Calculations:** Ratio-based problems are very common.
    *   **Example:** If orbital radius is doubled, period increases by `(2)^(3/2)` times.

2.  **Variation of Acceleration due to Gravity (g):**
    *   **Formulas:** `g_h = g(1 - 2h/R_E)` (approximation) and `g_d = g(1 - d/R_E)`.
    *   **Graphical Representation:** How `g` varies from the center to infinity (linear increase `g ∝ r` inside, inverse square `g ∝ 1/r²` outside).
    *   **Comparison:** `g` at height `h` vs. `g` at depth `d`.
    *   **Effect of Earth's Rotation:** `g' = g - ω²R_E cos²λ`. Understanding `g` at poles vs. equator.
    *   **Effect of Earth's Shape:** `g` at poles is greater than `g` at equator.

3.  **Gravitational Potential Energy (U) and Gravitational Potential (V):**
    *   **System of Particles:** Calculating potential energy for 3 or more particles (sum of all unique pairs).
    *   **Work Done:** Work done = `mΔV` or `ΔU`. Often involves moving a mass between two points.
    *   **Sign Convention:** Always negative for attractive forces, zero at infinity.
    *   **Relation to mgh:** When to use the approximation `mgh`.

4.  **Escape Speed (v_e):**
    *   **Formula:** `v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR)`.
    *   **Comparison:** Escape speeds from different planets/moons.
    *   **Energy Conservation:** If projected with speed `v > v_e`, calculating speed at infinity.
    *   **Conceptual:** Reason for atmosphere on planets.

5.  **Orbital Mechanics of Satellites:**
    *   **Orbital Velocity (v_o):** `v_o = √(GM/r)`. Comparison with `v_e` (`v_o = v_e/√2` near surface).
    *   **Time Period (T):** `T = 2π√(r³/GM)`.
    *   **Total Energy (E):** `E = -GMm/(2r)`.
    *   **Energy Relations (for circular orbit):** `E = -KE = PE/2`. Crucial for energy transfer problems.
    *   **Binding Energy:** `BE = -E`.
    *   **Geostationary Satellites:** Conditions, height, applications.

6.  **Spherical Shell Theorems:**
    *   **Force inside a hollow sphere is zero.**
    *   **Force outside a spherical body is as if mass is concentrated at center.**
    *   **Force inside a solid sphere (`F ∝ r`).**

7.  **Weightlessness:**
    *   **Conceptual understanding:** It's due to free fall, not zero gravity. Distinguish between true zero gravity and apparent weightlessness.

# CONCEPTUAL TRAPS

1.  **'g' vs. 'G':** Students often confuse the universal gravitational constant `G` (a constant everywhere) with acceleration due to gravity `g` (varies with location).
2.  **Distance `r`:** In `F = GMm/r²`, `r` is the distance between the *centers* of the masses. For problems involving heights/depths, remember `r = R_E + h` or `r = R_E - d`.
3.  **Sign of Potential Energy:** Gravitational potential energy `U = -GMm/r` is always negative. This indicates a bound system. Positive total energy means escape.
4.  **Approximation of `g_h`:** The formula `g_h ≈ g(1 - 2h/R_E)` is an approximation valid only for `h << R_E`. Do not use it for large heights. Use the exact formula `g_h = g [R_E / (R_E + h)]²`.
5.  **Uniform Density Assumption:** The formula `g_d = g(1 - d/R_E)` assumes uniform density of Earth. If density varies, the calculation changes (though NEET rarely asks for non-uniform density).
6.  **Gravitational Force Inside a Hollow Sphere:** It's **zero**. This is a classic trap. Do not calculate `GMm/r²`.
7.  **Mass in Kepler's Third Law:** In `T² = (4π² / GM) a³`, `M` is the mass of the *central* body (e.g., Sun for planets, Earth for satellites), not the orbiting body.
8.  **Weightlessness in Space:** It does **NOT** mean there's no gravity. It means the astronaut and the spacecraft are in continuous free fall together, experiencing no normal reaction force.
9.  **Escape Velocity vs. Orbital Velocity:**
    *   `v_e = √(2GM/R)`
    *   `v_o = √(GM/r)`
    *   They differ by a factor of `√2` (when `r=R`). Confusing them is a common mistake.
10. **Energy Relations for Circular Orbits:** `E = -KE = PE/2` is *only* true for circular orbits. For elliptical orbits, total energy is still constant and negative, but KE and PE vary, and these specific relations don't hold.
11. **Superposition Principle:** Remember to perform *vector* addition for forces and *scalar* addition for potentials.
12. **Units and Dimensions:** Always check units in formulas and final answers. `G`'s units `Nm²/kg²` are often forgotten.

# MEMORY BOOSTERS

1.  **Kepler's Laws Mnemonics:**
    *   **1st Law (Orbits):** "E for Ellipse, F for Foci." (Elliptical orbits, Sun at Foci)
    *   **2nd Law (Areas):** "A for Area, L for Angular Momentum." (Equal Areas in Equal Times due to Angular Momentum Conservation)
    *   **3rd Law (Periods):** "T-squared is a-cubed." (T² ∝ a³)

2.  **`g` Variation Analogies:**
    *   **"Up and Down, `g` goes Down!"**: For both height `(h)` and depth `(d)`, the value of `g` decreases from its maximum at the Earth's surface. Visualise `g` as a hump, highest at the surface.
    *   **Inside Earth (`g ∝ r`):** Imagine a string tied to the center of the Earth, extending to the surface. If you're inside, only the mass within the radius `r` pulls you, and the further out you go (closer to surface), the more mass pulls you, linearly. Like stretching a spring.
    *   **Outside Earth (`g ∝ 1/r²`):** The strength of a light bulb decreases with `1/r²`. Gravity similarly spreads out.

3.  **Gravitational Potential Energy (U) Sign:**
    *   "Gravity wants to pull things together. It's an attractive relationship. Negative means they're 'bound' together."
    *   Or, "Infinity is free (`U=0`). Anything less free is a 'debt' (negative)."

4.  **Escape Speed vs. Orbital Speed:**
    *   **`v_o = √(gR)` (Orbital):** "To *stay* in orbit, you need `gR`." (Pronounced "gee-arr").
    *   **`v_e = √(2gR)` (Escape):** "To *flee* and be free, you need *two* `gR`." (The `√2` factor is key).
    *   Visualise: `v_e` is faster than `v_o` by `√2` times. You need more speed to totally escape than to just keep falling around.

5.  **Satellite Energy Relations (for circular orbits):**
    *   "Kinetic is positive, Potential is double-negative, Total is single-negative."
    *   `KE = +X`
    *   `PE = -2X`
    *   `E = -X`
    *   So, `E = -KE = PE/2`. This relationship is crucial and saves time.

6.  **Gravitational Shielding:**
    *   "Gravity is stubborn! It can't be blocked. Like a ghost, it passes through everything."
    *   Contrast with electric fields, which *can* be shielded.

7.  **Cavendish Experiment:**
    *   "Cavendish's **G** for Giant Earth's Mass." (He measured G, which let us calculate Earth's mass).

# CHAPTER REVISION SHEET

## I. Historical & Kepler's Laws
*   **Galileo:** All bodies fall with constant acceleration `g`.
*   **Ptolemy:** Geocentric model.
*   **Aryabhatta/Copernicus:** Heliocentric model.
*   **Tycho Brahe:** Observational data.
*   **Kepler's Laws:**
    1.  **Orbits:** Elliptical, Sun at one focus. Perihelion (closest), Aphelion (farthest).
    2.  **Areas:** `dA/dt = L/(2m)` = constant. Consequence of `L` conservation (central force). Planet faster near Sun.
    3.  **Periods:** `T² ∝ a³`. `T² = (4π²/GM_central) a³`. `a` is semi-major axis.

## II. Newton's Law of Gravitation
*   **Statement:** `F ∝ m₁m₂/r²`.
*   **Formula:** `F = G m₁m₂/r²`. Always attractive. `F` is a central force.
*   **Vector form:** `**F**₁₂ = - (G m₁m₂/r²) **r̂**₁₂`.
*   **Superposition:** `**F**_net = Σ **F**_i` (vector sum).
*   **Gravitational Constant (G):** `6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m²/kg²`. Measured by Cavendish. `[M⁻¹L³T⁻²]`.
*   **Spherical Shell Theorems:**
    *   Outside: behaves as if mass is at center (`F = GMm/r²`).
    *   Inside hollow shell: `F = 0`.
    *   Inside solid sphere: `F ∝ r` (only mass within `r` pulls).

## III. Acceleration due to Gravity (g)
*   **Definition:** `g = F/m`. On Earth surface: `g = GM_E/R_E² ≈ 9.8 m/s²`.
*   **Variation with Height (h):**
    *   Exact: `g_h = GM_E / (R_E+h)² = g [R_E / (R_E+h)]²`.
    *   Approx (h << R_E): `g_h ≈ g(1 - 2h/R_E)`.
*   **Variation with Depth (d):** `g_d = g(1 - d/R_E)`. (Assumes uniform density). `g_d=0` at center.
*   **Overall:** `g` is max at surface, decreases up & down.
*   **Variation with Shape:** `R_P < R_E` => `g_P > g_E`.
*   **Variation with Rotation:** `g' = g - ω²R_E cos²λ`. `g` min at equator (λ=0), max at poles (λ=90).

## IV. Gravitational Potential Energy & Potential
*   **Conservative Force:** Gravity is conservative.
*   **Potential Energy (U):** Work done to bring mass from `∞` to `r`.
    *   `U = -GMm/r`. (Convention `U(∞)=0`).
    *   `ΔU = W_ext = -W_grav`.
    *   `U_mgh` approx: `ΔU ≈ mgh` for `h << R_E`.
    *   **System PE:** Sum of PE of all pairs. `U_total = Σ (-G m_i m_j / r_ij)`.
*   **Potential (V):** Potential energy per unit mass.
    *   `V = U/m = -GM/r`.
    *   Scalar quantity. `V(∞)=0`.
    *   `W = mΔV`.

## V. Escape Speed
*   **Definition:** Minimum speed to escape gravity (`KE_f = 0` at `∞`).
*   **Formula:** `v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR)`.
*   `v_e(Earth) ≈ 11.2 km/s`.
*   Independent of projectile mass, direction. Depends on planet's M, R.
*   `v > v_e`: Object reaches `∞` with residual KE.

## VI. Earth Satellites
*   **Orbital Velocity (Circular):** `v_o = √(GM/r)`. `r = R_E + h`.
    *   Close to surface (h≈0): `v_o = √(gR_E) ≈ 7.92 km/s`.
*   **Time Period (Circular):** `T = 2π√(r³/GM)`. `T² ∝ r³`.
*   **Energy (Circular Orbit):**
    *   `KE = GMm/(2r)` (positive)
    *   `PE = -GMm/r` (negative)
    *   `E = KE + PE = -GMm/(2r)` (negative).
    *   **Relations:** `E = -KE = PE/2`.
*   **Binding Energy:** `BE = -E = GMm/(2r)`. Energy required to escape.
*   **Geostationary Satellite:** `T=24h`, equatorial plane, same direction as Earth's rotation, `h ≈ 36,000 km`.
*   **Weightlessness:** Not zero gravity, but continuous free-fall of astronaut & satellite together.

# TOP 25 NEET QUESTIONS

### Easy Questions (10)

1.  **Question:** According to Kepler's first law, planets revolve around the Sun in:
    (a) Circular orbits
    (b) Elliptical orbits with Sun at the center
    (c) Elliptical orbits with Sun at one focus
    (d) Parabolic orbits
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Kepler's First Law (Law of Orbits) explicitly states that planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one of the two foci.

2.  **Question:** The unit of gravitational constant 'G' is:
    (a) Nm/kg²
    (b) Nm²/kg
    (c) Nm²/kg²
    (d) Nkg²/m²
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** From `F = GMm/r²`, rearrange to `G = Fr²/Mm`. Units: `N * m² / (kg * kg) = Nm²/kg²`.

3.  **Question:** If the distance between two point masses is doubled, the gravitational force between them will become:
    (a) Half
    (b) Double
    (c) One-fourth
    (d) Four times
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Gravitational force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance (`F ∝ 1/r²`). If `r` doubles, `r²` becomes `4r²`, so `F` becomes `1/4` of its original value.

4.  **Question:** A body is taken from the Earth's surface to a height equal to the radius of the Earth. The acceleration due to gravity `g'` at that height will be:
    (a) `g/2`
    (b) `g/4`
    (c) `g`
    (d) `2g`
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** At height `h=R_E`, the distance from Earth's center is `r = R_E + h = R_E + R_E = 2R_E`.
    `g' = GM_E / r² = GM_E / (2R_E)² = GM_E / (4R_E²) = (1/4) * (GM_E / R_E²) = g/4`.

5.  **Question:** The work done to move a unit mass from infinity to a point in a gravitational field is called:
    (a) Gravitational Potential Energy
    (b) Gravitational Potential
    (c) Escape Energy
    (d) Binding Energy
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** Gravitational potential is defined as the work done per unit mass in bringing a test mass from infinity to a point in the gravitational field.

6.  **Question:** What is the gravitational force on a point mass inside a uniform spherical shell?
    (a) `GMm/r²`
    (b) `GMm/R²`
    (c) Zero
    (d) Varies with distance from center
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** According to the spherical shell theorem, the gravitational force on a point mass placed inside a uniform spherical shell is zero.

7.  **Question:** The orbital speed of a satellite revolving very close to the Earth's surface is `v_o`. The escape speed from the Earth's surface is `v_e`. What is the relation between them?
    (a) `v_e = 2v_o`
    (b) `v_e = √2 v_o`
    (c) `v_o = √2 v_e`
    (d) `v_e = v_o`
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** `v_o = √(GM/R)` and `v_e = √(2GM/R)`. So, `v_e = √2 * √(GM/R) = √2 v_o`.

8.  **Question:** If the total mechanical energy of an orbiting satellite is `E`, its kinetic energy `KE` is:
    (a) `E`
    (b) `-E`
    (c) `2E`
    (d) `-2E`
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** For a satellite in a circular orbit, the total mechanical energy `E = -KE`. Therefore, `KE = -E`.

9.  **Question:** An astronaut in an orbiting satellite experiences weightlessness because:
    (a) There is no gravity in space.
    (b) The satellite is far from Earth.
    (c) Both the astronaut and satellite are in free fall.
    (d) The astronaut has zero mass in space.
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Weightlessness is the apparent absence of weight due to the continuous free-fall condition, not the absence of gravity.

10. **Question:** If the Earth were to shrink suddenly, keeping its mass constant, the value of 'g' on its surface would:
    (a) Increase
    (b) Decrease
    (c) Remain unchanged
    (d) Become zero
    **Answer:** (a)
    **Explanation:** `g = GM/R²`. If `M` is constant and `R` decreases, then `g` would increase.

### Medium Questions (10)

11. **Question:** The ratio of the periods of two planets A and B revolving around the Sun is `T_A : T_B = 8 : 1`. The ratio of their orbital radii `R_A : R_B` will be:
    (a) `4 : 1`
    (b) `1 : 4`
    (c) `2 : 1`
    (d) `1 : 2`
    **Answer:** (a)
    **Explanation:** From Kepler's Third Law, `T² ∝ R³`.
    `(T_A / T_B)² = (R_A / R_B)³`
    `(8/1)² = (R_A / R_B)³`
    `64 = (R_A / R_B)³`
    `R_A / R_B = ³√64 = 4/1`. So, `R_A : R_B = 4 : 1`.

12. **Question:** A body weighs 60 N on the surface of the Earth. How much will it weigh at a height `h = R_E / 2` above the Earth's surface?
    (a) `40 N`
    (b) `26.7 N`
    (c) `30 N`
    (d) `15 N`
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** Weight `W = mg`. On surface `W = 60 N`.
    At height `h = R_E/2`, `r = R_E + h = R_E + R_E/2 = (3/2)R_E`.
    `g_h = g [R_E / (R_E + h)]² = g [R_E / (3/2 R_E)]² = g (2/3)² = 4g/9`.
    New weight `W' = m g_h = m (4g/9) = (4/9) * (mg) = (4/9) * 60 N = 240/9 N ≈ 26.67 N`.

13. **Question:** A satellite is revolving in a circular orbit at a height `h` from the Earth's surface. If the radius of the Earth is `R`, the speed of the satellite is proportional to:
    (a) `(R+h)`
    (b) `(R+h)^-1`
    (c) `(R+h)^-1/2`
    (d) `(R+h)^-3/2`
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Orbital speed `v_o = √(GM/r)`. Here, `r = R+h`. So `v_o = √(GM/(R+h)) = (GM)^(1/2) * (R+h)^(-1/2)`. Thus, `v_o ∝ (R+h)^(-1/2)`.

14. **Question:** Three particles each of mass `M` are placed at the vertices of an equilateral triangle of side `L`. The gravitational potential at the centroid of the triangle is:
    (a) `-3GM/L`
    (b) `-9GM/L`
    (c) `-3√3 GM/L`
    (d) `-6GM/L`
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Let `r` be the distance from a vertex to the centroid. For an equilateral triangle, `r = L/√3`.
    The potential at the centroid is the scalar sum of potentials due to each mass:
    `V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ = -GM/r - GM/r - GM/r = -3GM/r`
    `V = -3GM / (L/√3) = -3√3 GM/L`.

15. **Question:** An object is projected from the Earth's surface with a speed `v = 2v_e`, where `v_e` is the escape speed. What is its speed when it is very far away from the Earth?
    (a) `v_e`
    (b) `√2 v_e`
    (c) `√3 v_e`
    (d) `2v_e`
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:** Using conservation of energy: `KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f`
    `(1/2)mv² - GMm/R = (1/2)mv_f² + 0`
    `(1/2)m(2v_e)² - GMm/R = (1/2)mv_f²`
    `(1/2)m(4v_e²) - GMm/R = (1/2)mv_f²`
    Since `v_e² = 2GM/R`, then `GMm/R = (1/2)mv_e²`.
    `(1/2)m(4v_e²) - (1/2)mv_e² = (1/2)mv_f²`
    `4(1/2)mv_e² - (1/2)mv_e² = (1/2)mv_f²`
    `3(1/2)mv_e² = (1/2)mv_f²`
    `v_f² = 3v_e²` => `v_f = √3 v_e`.

16. **Question:** If the Earth stops rotating, the value of 'g' at the equator will:
    (a) Increase
    (b) Decrease
    (c) Remain same
    (d) Become zero
    **Answer:** (a)
    **Explanation:** The effective acceleration due to gravity is `g' = g - ω²R_E cos²λ`. At the equator, `λ=0`, so `g' = g - ω²R_E`. If the Earth stops rotating, `ω=0`, so `g'` becomes `g`. Since `g - ω²R_E < g`, stopping rotation will *increase* the effective 'g' at the equator.

17. **Question:** A satellite is launched into a circular orbit at a height `R_E` from the Earth's surface. The energy required to launch it from the Earth's surface (ignoring air resistance) is: (Mass of Earth `M_E`, mass of satellite `m`, radius of Earth `R_E`)
    (a) `GM_E m / (4R_E)`
    (b) `GM_E m / (2R_E)`
    (c) `GM_E m / R_E`
    (d) `3GM_E m / (4R_E)`
    **Answer:** (d)
    **Explanation:**
    Initial Energy on surface (`r = R_E`): `E_i = -GM_E m / R_E`. (Assuming it starts from rest, only PE).
    Final Energy in orbit (`r = R_E + R_E = 2R_E`): `E_f = -GM_E m / (2r) = -GM_E m / (2 * 2R_E) = -GM_E m / (4R_E)`.
    Energy required = `E_f - E_i = (-GM_E m / (4R_E)) - (-GM_E m / R_E) = GM_E m * (1 - 1/4) / R_E = 3GM_E m / (4R_E)`.

18. **Question:** The gravitational potential at a point on the surface of the Earth is `V`. The gravitational potential at the center of the Earth (assuming uniform density) is:
    (a) `V/2`
    (b) `V`
    (c) `3V/2`
    (d) `2V`
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:**
    Potential at surface `V_S = -GM/R`.
    Potential at center `V_C = -GM/R (3/2 - (R²/2R²)) = -GM/R (3/2) = (3/2) V_S`.
    So, `V_C = 3V/2`.

19. **Question:** A geostationary satellite orbits at a height of approximately 36,000 km from the Earth's surface. What is its orbital period?
    (a) 12 hours
    (b) 24 hours
    (c) 36 hours
    (d) 48 hours
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** A geostationary satellite by definition has an orbital period equal to the Earth's rotational period, which is 24 hours.

20. **Question:** A solid sphere of uniform density and radius `R` has a spherical cavity of radius `R/2` as shown in the figure (cavity tangent to surface). The gravitational field at the center of the cavity is:
    (a) `GM/(R²) `
    (b) `GM/(2R²) `
    (c) `GM/(8R²) `
    (d) `GM/(4R²) `
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:** (Requires vector addition and concept of superposition)
    Let the solid sphere be `S` (mass `M`, radius `R`). Let the cavity be `C` (radius `R/2`).
    The gravitational field of a sphere is `E = G * M * r / R³` for `r < R`.
    Consider the complete sphere without cavity (density `ρ`). Its mass `M = (4/3)πR³ρ`.
    The cavity is a sphere of radius `R/2` with negative mass `m' = (4/3)π(R/2)³ρ = (1/8) * (4/3)πR³ρ = M/8`.
    The center of the cavity is at `r = R/2` from the center of the large sphere.
    Gravitational field at cavity center `P` due to full sphere (without cavity) `E_full = G M (R/2) / R³ = GM / (2R²)`.
    Gravitational field at `P` due to the removed (negative) mass `m'` (center at `P`) is `0` as it's at its own center.
    Thus, the net field at the center of the cavity `E_net = E_full = GM / (2R²)`.
    Direction is towards the center of the original solid sphere.

### Hard Questions (5)

21. **Question:** Two satellites of masses `m₁` and `m₂` are orbiting the Earth in circular orbits of radii `r₁` and `r₂` respectively. If `m₁ > m₂` and `r₁ < r₂`, which of the following statements is true?
    (a) `v₁ < v₂`
    (b) `KE₁ > KE₂`
    (c) `T₁ > T₂`
    (d) `E₁ > E₂` (where `E` is total energy)
    **Answer:** (b)
    **Explanation:**
    *   **Orbital Velocity:** `v = √(GM/r)`. Since `r₁ < r₂`, `v₁ > v₂`. So (a) is false.
    *   **Kinetic Energy:** `KE = GMm/(2r)`. Since `m₁ > m₂` and `r₁ < r₂`, it's possible for `KE₁` to be greater than `KE₂`. Example: Let `m₁=2m, r₁=R` and `m₂=m, r₂=2R`. Then `KE₁ = GM(2m)/(2R) = GMm/R`. `KE₂ = GM(m)/(2*2R) = GMm/(4R)`. Here `KE₁ > KE₂`. So (b) is true.
    *   **Time Period:** `T = 2π√(r³/GM)`. Since `r₁ < r₂`, `T₁ < T₂`. So (c) is false.
    *   **Total Energy:** `E = -GMm/(2r)`. Since `r₁ < r₂`, `|E₁| > |E₂|`. As energy is negative, `E₁ < E₂`. So (d) is false.
    *   Therefore, the only statement that can be true is (b).

22. **Question:** Four particles, each of mass `m`, are placed at the vertices of a square of side `a`. The gravitational potential energy of the system is:
    (a) `- (4Gm²/a) (1 + 1/√2)`
    (b) `- (Gm²/a) (4 + 1/√2)`
    (c) `- (Gm²/a) (4 + √2)`
    (d) `- (4Gm²/a) (1 + √2)`
    **Answer:** (c)
    **Explanation:**
    There are 4 pairs of masses at distance `a` (sides).
    There are 2 pairs of masses at distance `a√2` (diagonals).
    Total potential energy `U = Σ U_ij`
    `U = 4 * (-Gm²/a) + 2 * (-Gm²/(a√2))`
    `U = -4Gm²/a - 2Gm²/(a√2)`
    `U = -Gm²/a * (4 + 2/√2)`
    `U = -Gm²/a * (4 + √2)`

24. **Question:** Two stars of masses `M` and `4M` are at a distance `d` apart. The neutral point (where net gravitational force is zero) is at a distance:
    (a) `d/3` from `M`
    (b) `d/2` from `M`
    (c) `2d/3` from `M`
    (d) `d/4` from `4M`
    **Answer:** (a)
    **Explanation:** Let the neutral point be at distance `x` from mass `M`. Then its distance from `4M` is `(d-x)`.
    At the neutral point, `F_M = F_{4M}`.
    `G M m / x² = G (4M) m / (d-x)²`
    `1/x² = 4/(d-x)²`
    `(d-x)² = 4x²`
    Taking square root: `d-x = ±2x`
    Case 1: `d-x = 2x` => `d = 3x` => `x = d/3`. This point is between the masses.
    Case 2: `d-x = -2x` => `d = -x` => `x = -d`. This point is outside, to the left of `M`. Since gravity is attractive, the neutral point must be between the masses.
    So, `x = d/3` from `M`.

25. **Question:** A satellite is revolving around the Earth. If its kinetic energy is doubled, then:
    (a) It will escape to infinity.
    (b) It will continue in the same orbit.
    (c) Its orbit will become elliptical.
    (d) It will fall to Earth.
    **Answer:** (a)
    **Explanation:** For a circular orbit, `KE = GMm/(2r)` and `E = -GMm/(2r) = -KE`.
    If `KE` is doubled to `2KE`, the new total energy `E'` would be `E' = 2KE + PE = 2KE + (-2KE) = 0`.
    A total energy of zero means the satellite has just enough energy to escape to infinity. So it will escape.
    *(Note: If KE were increased but not doubled, the orbit would become elliptical; if KE reduced, it falls; if KE exactly doubled, it escapes)*.

# FINAL TAKEAWAYS

If you remember only these 10 things from this chapter, you'll be well-prepared for many NEET questions:

1.  **Kepler's Laws:** Planets move in elliptical orbits (`T² ∝ a³`), sweep equal areas in equal times (due to `L` conservation).
2.  **Newton's Law:** `F = GMm/r²`, always attractive, central force.
3.  **Gravitational Constant 'G':** Universal constant (`6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ Nm²/kg²`), measured by Cavendish.
4.  **Acceleration due to Gravity 'g':** `g = GM/R²` on surface. Maximum at surface, decreases with height `(1-2h/R)` and depth `(1-d/R)`. It's higher at poles than equator (`g_P > g_E`).
5.  **Gravitational Potential Energy (U):** `U = -GMm/r`. Always negative, defined as zero at infinity.
6.  **Gravitational Potential (V):** `V = -GM/r`. Scalar quantity, potential energy per unit mass.
7.  **Escape Speed:** `v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR)`. Minimum speed to leave gravity, independent of projectile mass.
8.  **Orbital Speed:** `v_o = √(GM/r)`. Speed to maintain circular orbit. (`v_e = √2 v_o` near surface).
9.  **Satellite Energy (Circular Orbit):** `E = -GMm/(2r)`. Total energy is negative, and `E = -KE = PE/2`.
10. **Weightlessness:** It's due to free-fall, not absence of gravity. Gravitational shielding is not possible.
